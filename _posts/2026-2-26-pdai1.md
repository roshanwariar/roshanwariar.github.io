Automation has always been integral to chip implementation. I saw this firsthand has a physical design engineer. Some of my older coworkers remembered a time where implementation of a block included a large number of instances that had to be hand placed and routed (PnR). And while there is some custom PnR still happening, it’s in special circumstances with a small number of instances. Since then, designs have grown tremendously in gate count and cutting edge tech nodes have ridiculously complex design rules, so it’s not suprisingly that the design tools have adapted to leverage algorithms to automate PnR under the guidance and constraints of an implementation engineer. The algorithms that power the tools from companies like Cadence and Synopsys are apart of a field known as electronic design automation.<br> 

It is only natural then that these vendors would try and incorporate AI in their EDA tools to increase efficiency in block execution. After all, runtimes from synthesis to signoff are at least a week, most designs still need extensive ECOs to meet tapeout requirements, and poor PPA predictability at early stages of the design flow can lead to extra trials prior to convergence. What is a little surprising, but also exciting, is that fabless chip companies (ie Nvidia and Google) have also entered the space of developing their own custom AI EDA tools to accelerate implementation timelines.<br> 

![Design Flow](/assets/images/PhysicalDesign.png)

The most famous example is probably Google’s [AlphaChip](https://deepmind.google/blog/how-alphachip-transformed-computer-chip-design/). I’ll save the details of this model for a later part of this series, but simply, it uses a graph neural network to get an intuition of macro placement during floorplanning by looking at completed designs, and then uses a reinforcement learning algorithm to place the macros for new designs. AlphaChip treats placements as a game with macros as pieces it has to place on a board under certain constraints and with a score connected to power/performance factors such as wirelength. In this way, it is similar to AlphaGo (floorplanning represents a much larger search space however). Minimizing wirelength helps reduce dynamic power through quadratically lower resistance, fewer buffers, better timing/crosstalk, and higher area utilization. Trillim, Google's 6th generation TPU, is claimed to have 25 blocks that had macro placement done by AlphaChip with about 6% better wirelength and only taking hours for the model compared to weeks for a human designer.<br>

In addition to the types of models that can actually produce finished designs, there are attempts to incorporate AI to increase the speed of steps in the physical design flow. For example, Nvidia’s [TransSizer](https://research.nvidia.com/publication/2022-10_transsizer-novel-transformer-based-fast-gate-sizer) is used to making gate sizing a netlist in synthesis orders of magnitude faster. It does this by treating prediction of gate sizes like a language translation problem. In NLP, the SOTA for language translation are BERT-style models like this [paper](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1810.04805). TransSizer uses a similar bidirectional transformer architecture to translate a placed unoptimized netlist to a netlist with drive strengths. Uniquely, the encoder feeds to two different decoders: one that looks at the startpoint to the endpoint of a path and vice versa. Two decoders are needed because gate sizing affects the timing and power of both the next and previous stage.<br> 

![TransSizer](/assets/images/bert_sizing.png)

This post is going to focus on the latter type of AI model for physical design. Specifically, I want to look at a paper I came across at the 2024 Hot Chip Conference; [MAVIREC](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2012.10597): ML-Aided Vectored IR-Drop Estimation and Classification from Nvidia and the University of Minnesota. When large cells are densely packed together or a cell is far from nearby power grid, you can have an IR violation. Here IR drop refers to the difference in voltage between what is supplied $$V_{DD}$$ and what is received at the standard cell $$V_{DD} - I*R$$. All the gate sizes and placements are done with specific timing and power constraints, but the underlying assumption early in the design flow is that each cell gets the ideal supply voltage $$V_{DD}$$. IR drop is localized like a heat map because it is correlated with cell drive and density thus manifesting like a heat map below. This means that certain sections of the chip operate with different speeds than predicted which could to catastrophic failures. Switching activity indicates how often a cell pin is switching on and off given a particular timing window, and if there is a lot of localized switching activity, it can worsen transient IR drops. This is called dynamic IR analysis. These violations are easy to fix once identified (adding power grid straps or moving/downsizing cells with positive timing margin), but this process is often lengthy.<br>

![IR Drop Ex](/assets/images/IR_Drop_2.jpg) 

Before jumping into the deep learning side of things, it’s important to understand how a typical vendor’s tool would do dynamic IR drop analysis. There are several input files that each detail important characteristics of your design that are fed to the IR analysis tool. Switching activity is profiled via “vectors” and stored in a .vcd file. You need a list of gate instances and their placements stored in the .vg and .def files respectively. The .lef file helps complete the layout information with technology specific cell layout and metal definitions such as width, spacing, resistance, and capacitance. A Liberty file, .lib, contains a non-linear delay model (NLDM) that sets the cell delay for every cell based on the transition of the input signal as well as the output load that the cell has to drive. The file also informs the internal and leakage power of the cell. After the design is placed and routed, you do a parasitic extraction step to get the RC values of all the nets in the design. This is contained in a .spef file and passed to the IR analysis tool. Finally, a timing window file, .twf, is used to determine when pins are likely to switch. This seems redundant with the .vcd, but the .vcd looks at logical switching, whereas the timing window is based on a static timing analysis and provides a better worst-case scenario.<br> 

![IR Flow](/assets/images/IR_Flow.png)

Let’s look at the typical IR analysis flow now that all the right files have been submitted. Out of the 100k cycles of switching activity in the .vcd file, only a handful of short slices (~20 cycles) are chosen by the vector profiler based on worst case average power. This is a very slow process - around 2 hours to grab the top 3 slices from a vector with 5000 slices on a design with 3 million instances. This process is not even exact. Heuristics are used to select slices, so often times, more pessimissitic slices crucial for analysis are missed. Once the slices are processed, the output is a heat map of the IR drop per instance in the design.<br> 

MAVIREC works by first extracting useful features per instance from the input files. These are things like switching/internal/leakage power ($$p_s, p_i, p_l$$), distance to via stack in a set radius ($$r^{-1} = \sum_{i}^{V} d_{i}^{-1}$$ or effective distance $$d$$ from the $$i$$-th via stack of $$V$$ via stacks where all via stacks are $$d\leq 5\mu$$m), and average toggle rate of instance $$i$$ in a particular slice $$\tau_i$$. This is done using standard flow tools. Then an ML-based recommender system finds hundreds of candidate slices instead of just a handful from the previous heuristic. The paper goes into details of this algorithm, but for the scope of this project I will skip it.<br> 

A few more features are derived by combining the above values:<br> 
Total Power: $$p_{tot} = p_l + p_s + p_i$$<br> 
Toggle Rate Adjusted Total Power: $$p_{r} = p_l + \tau_i(p_s + p_i)$$<br>

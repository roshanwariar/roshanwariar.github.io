Automation has always been integral to chip implementation. I saw this firsthand has a physical design engineer. Some of my older coworkers remembered a time where implementation of a block included a large number of instances that had to be hand placed and routed (PnR). And while there is some custom PnR still happening, it’s in special circumstances with a small number of instances. Since then, designs have grown tremendously in gate count and cutting edge tech nodes have ridiculously complex design rules, so it’s not suprisingly that the design tools have adapted to leverage algorithms to automate PnR under the guidance and constraints of an implementation engineer. The algorithms that power the tools from companies like Cadence and Synopsys are apart of a field known as electronic design automation.<br> 

It is only natural then that these vendors would try and incorporate AI in their EDA tools to increase efficiency in block execution. After all, runtimes from synthesis to signoff are at least a week, most designs still need extensive ECOs to meet tapeout requirements, and poor PPA predictability at early stages of the design flow can lead to extra trials prior to convergence. What is a little surprisingly, but also exciting, is that fabless chip companies (ie Nvidia and Google) have also entered the space of developing their own custom AI EDA tools to accelerate implementation timelines.<br> 

![Design Flow](/assets/images/PhysicalDesign.png)

The most famous example is probably Google’s [AlphaChip](https://deepmind.google/blog/how-alphachip-transformed-computer-chip-design/). I’ll save the details of this model for a later part of this series, but simply, it uses a graph neural network to get an intuition of macro placement during floorplanning by looking at completed designs, and then uses a reinforcement learning algorithm to place the macros for new designs. AlphaChip treats placements as a game with macros as pieces it has to place on a board under certain constraints and with a score connected to power/performance factors such as wirelength. In this way, it is similar to AlphaGo (floorplanning represents a much larger search space however). Minimizing wirelength helps reduce dynamic power through quadratically lower resistance, fewer buffers, better timing/crosstalk, and higher area utilization. Trillim, Google's 6th generation TPU, is claimed to have 25 blocks that had macro placement done by AlphaChip with about 6% better wirelength and only taking hours for the model compared to weeks for a human designer.<br>

In addition to the types of models that can actually produce finished designs, there are attempts to incorporate AI to increase the speed of steps in the physical design flow. For example, Nvidia’s [TransSizer](https://research.nvidia.com/publication/2022-10_transsizer-novel-transformer-based-fast-gate-sizer) is used to making gate sizing a netlist in synthesis orders of magnitude faster. It does this by treating prediction of gate sizes like a language translation problem. In NLP, the SOTA for language translation are BERT-style models like this [paper](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1810.04805). TransSizer uses a similar bidirectional transformer architecture to translate a placed unoptimized netlist to a netlist with drive strengths. Uniquely, the encoder feeds to two different decoders: one that looks at the startpoint to the endpoint of a path and vice versa. Two decoders are needed because gate sizing affects the timing and power of both the next and previous stage.<br>

~~<w>

My earliest memories of being interested in computers date back to investigating the latest and greatest "tech specs" to come out of current smartphone or gaming laptop release. At the same time in high school, I had a curisoity in reading about physics I barely understood. Both of these pasttimes seemed to inevitably lead me to study electrical engineering (at the University of Minnesota) and focus on semiconductor physics and circuit design during my master's degree. While I was still incredibly fascinated by how specific computer chips could enable all sorts of multimedia features like computational photography, I had grown much more attached to the idea of leverging algorithms and circuits to simulate reality itself like testing a car's aerodynamics in a virtual wind tunnel to improve its design or understanding the soft matter phase transitions of a polymer one might use for drug discovery. The field of scientific computing enamored me for reasons both practical and philosophical.<w> 

Pratically, I was attracted to the breadth of impact better chips for simulation could have. Making computers more efficient could allow engineers to rapidly iterate on designs in silico leading to more efficient transportation, infrastructure (the bridge and road kind), and ultimately better computer chips. Scientists would also enjoy the ability to run experiments in simulations giving them the opportunity understand behaviors with strong predictive power transforming fields like material science and biochemistry. Philosophically, these fields are plagued by strongly emergent systems whereby properties of the system cannot be inferred from simple interactions of the its consituent parts. This irreducible complexity is especially prominent in biology; parts cannot be studied in isolation because its dynamics are wildy different based on its interactions with the environment. But, scientific computing gives us a way to potentially study these systems in a more controlled way by simulating the dynamics of all parts and reproducing the emergences naturally in a computer. But even for the most trivial molecular dynamics simulations, you need a supercomputer running for days to get results similar to an experiment in a wet lab.<w>

So while computers to tackle the philosophical problem of studying emergent systems, we don't have a solution to make them fast enough to study these systems at useful sizes. Computers need to be orders of magnitudes faster before we can start designing new drugs, biofuels, transport, etc. While Moore might be dead, transistors are still getting smaller and faster, but it may take decades to simply rely on the 20-30% performance you get from a new transistor node and another 10-15% you get from microarchitectural improvements. As many people have pointed out, what if we need to rethink computing from the ground up across its entire stack?<w>

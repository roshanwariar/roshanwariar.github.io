My earliest memories of being interested in computers date back to investigating the latest and greatest "tech specs" to come out of the current smartphone or gaming laptop release. At the same time in high school, I loved reading about physics. Both of these pasttimes seemed to inevitably lead me to study electrical engineering (at the University of Minnesota) and focus on semiconductor physics and circuit design during my master's degree. While I was still fascinated by how specific computer chips could enable all sorts of multimedia features like computational photography, I was drawn to the idea of leverging algorithms and circuits to simulate the dynamics of the world around us like testing a car's aerodynamics in a virtual wind tunnel to improve its design or understanding the soft matter phase transitions of a polymer one might use for drug discovery. The field of scientific computing enamored me for reasons both practical and philosophical.<w> 

Practically, I was attracted to the breadth of impact better chips for simulation could have. Making computers faster could allow engineers to rapidly iterate on designs in silico leading to more efficient transportation, infrastructure, and ultimately better computer chips. Scientists would also enjoy the ability to run experiments in simulations giving them the opportunity understand behaviors with stronger predictive power transforming fields like material science and biochemistry. These fields are plagued by strongly emergent systems which I was fascinated with when I was first introduced to them in my high school's philosophy course (shout out Mr. Benolkin). Emergent systems have properties that cannot be inferred from simple interactions of its consituent parts. This irreducible complexity is especially prominent in biology; parts cannot be studied in isolation because its dynamics are wildy different based on its interactions with the environment. But, scientific computing gives us a way to study these systems in a more controlled setting by reproducing emergent behavior through faithful simulation of the system dynamics. But even for the most trivial molecular dynamics simulations, you need a supercomputer running for days to get results similar to an experiment in a wet lab.<w>

So while computer simulations give a workaround the philosophical problem of studying emergent systems, we don't have a solution to make them fast enough to study these systems at useful sizes. Computers need to be orders of magnitude faster before we can start designing new drugs, biofuels, transport, etc. While Moore's Law might be dead, transistors are still getting faster and smaller, but it may take decades to solely rely on the 20-30% performance you get from a new transistor node and another 10-15% you get from microarchitectural improvements to get the performance for practical simulations. As many people have pointed out, what if we need to rethink computing from the ground up -- from atoms to algorithms.<w>

The history of computing is somewhat ironic. Early analog computers were efficient at simulating differential equations because their components can embed the physics of the problem into the physics of current and voltage passing through various components like resistors, capacitors, potentiometers, and amplifiers. This meant that simulating physical systems like a spring-mass, which was difficult to solve with traditional methods and governed by the second-order differential equation $$my'' + dy' + cy = mg$$ could be written as $$y'' = -\frac{d}{m}y' - \frac{c}{m}y - \frac{g}{m}$$ and mapped to the 'analog computer' below. <w> 

![Analog computer](/assets/images/Federpendel_als_Analogrechenschaltung.png)

There were a few main issues with this approach to computing. First is susceptability to noise. If you built a multiplier out of analog components and tried to calculate 3*4, the computer would output 12.001 or 11.999 volts, not 12. And because analog components have variations in their operation due to external factors, you could see different answers depending on the room temperature or exposure to radiation. While approximate computing is acceptable in some modern applications, scientists and engineers in the early days of computing wanted processors that could perform precise measurements for long, complex arithmetic. Small errors on analog computers would compound in a way that makes them unsuitable for this task. Another big issue is related to resolution. Now it's true that analog computers can represent values to infinite precision, however, its dynamic range is poor. When the iPhone 5s was unveiled over a decade ago, it was one of the first smartphones to utilize a 64-bit processor. This double floating point representation has a dynamic range of 1500dB - an order of magnitude better than the best modern operational amplifiers on the market.<w>

With all of this information, it's no suprise that binary formats and digital logic took off. It was proven that logic gates could implement any mathematical function, they were far more robust against noise than their analog counterparts, and their stability allowed for those circuits to be made at ever smaller sizes. Suddenly, we had computers that we could do our taxes on, write text documents, and play video games. But with that generality and programmability, a lot of efficiency was lost. <w> 

![ADC](/assets/images/1682789114339.png)

Let's walk through the layers of abstraction in the compute stack. Take the differential equation shown above. An analog computer to implement a solution to that function would need somewhere in the range of 100-150 transistors. A digital circuit to get comparable results would need to implement a numerical method to solve the equation. To get good stability you could implement the fourth-order Runge-Kutta method which requires several floating point multipliers, dividers, adders, registers to hold intermediate values, control logic and a clock. This is easily hundreds of thousands of transistors. Surpringly, both methods have more or less equal area costs since digital transistors are much smaller than analog components. Power is a similar story, using low-voltage op-amps gets the analog implementation to be roughly in the same ballpark compared to the digital computer. Speed is where analog shines. To get the answer from the digital circuit, you need to compute $$y$$ for each time step $$t$$ which is 100-200 clock cycles. This could be anywhere from 0.1-1us per step (not to mention readout using an DAC) whereas an analog circuit would immediately solve the equation and start outputting valid samples. As you scale digital computers up, they end up spending more than 80% of their time and energy shuffling data back and forth from memory instead of actually performing calculations.<w>  

So the irony is that although we started with analog computers for simulating physics and abandoned them for issues regarding range and precision, we are now at a point because of the scale of deep learning and scientific computing algorithms where analog computers might start making sense over digital. But, I don't think we are simply reviving analog computers of the past. Then, people were trying to build generalized, programmable machines to cover a wide variety of different computational tasks. The story is different today. We want specialized machines that can't run Excel, but can solve a group of problems whose size and complexity far exceed what is accomplishable through standard scaling of classical computers. Ultimately, it's a realization that many of our toughest algorithms are in fact synonymous with physical processes, that nature and the laws of physics are potentially the most efficient computers we have observed, and that _physics-based computing_ might be the ticket to unlocking far more computational power than currently available.<w>

![Bloch Sphere](/assets/images/Bloch_sphere.svg.png)

Perhaps the most well-known and longest running example of physics-based computing is quantum computing. As the name suggests, this kind of computer utilizes two key properties from quantum mechanics to perform massively parallel computations. These are quantum superposition and entanglement. The spin of a particle when measured is usually a binary value (a quantum bit or qubit), but prior to measurement the spin can take on a value that is some linear combination of its two readout states. This is superposition. On it's own it isn't very useful, but when paired with engtanglement it allows for immense information density. For example, a single electron's spin when in superposition requires 2 numbers to describe its state - one for the likelihood it will be measured in a 'up' state and one it is measured in a 'down' state. Two entangled electrons have the follwing possible measurements outcomes: 00, 01, 10, 11. Therefore, you need 4 numbers to describe 2 entangled electrons, 8 for 3 electrons, 16 for 4, etc. The entanglement is treated as a single object which can then be sent through a series of quantum gates to evaluate a function. But instead of getting one answer at the output, you would have essentially evaluated the function for all possible states ($$2^\text{num of qubits}$$). So instead of a digital computer where additions of memory or cores lead to linear speedups on parallelizable code, quantum computers offer exponential speedups with only linear increases in qubits.<w> 

This dramatically increases the search capabilities in optimization algorithms. Protein folding is one of the best examples. Cyrus Levinthal in 1969 pointed out that there were so many degrees of freedom in an unfolded string of amino acids that it would take a supercomputer (even with today's computational capabilities) longer than the age of the universe to search through each possible 3D structure and find the one with the minimum energy (the one the chain will eventually fold into). Yet, nature folds proteins incredibly fast even for complex structures, indicating that physics finds a way to minimize the energy efficiently. Now, it's possible to simulate the quantum mechanics on a digital computer, but the Hilbert space of the wavefunction grows exponentially with the number of electrons meaning that by the time we want to do quantum-level simulations of protein folding of any reasonable size protein, we would need an unreasonable amount of compute/energy/time. Instead, we can map the Hamiltonian (total energy of the system) to qubits which naturally embed wavefunctions allowing us to perform protein folding with a linear number of qubits. IonQ and Kipu Quantum were able to get a 12 amino acid protein folding problem solved on a 36 qubit quantum computer just last year (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.07866). Typically, most proteins are hundreds of amino acid blocks.<w> 

You might be thinking that AlphaFold already does this - that's true to an extent. A lot of AI algorithms can be seen as efficient search over a solution space that is guided from training data, and in this case is evolutionary information about proteins. The main problem is generalization. Although Demis Hassabis has claimed both AlphaFold and world models like Veo may be able to learn the complex nonlinear dynamics of proteins and fluids respectively, it seems that these models don't learn generalizable equations of the dynamics but rather interpolate on a their training data manifold. This is shown when AlphaFold's performance deteriorates for intrisincally disordered proteins (IDPs - 30 to 40% of the human proteome). The argument could be made that since they are not trained on these proteins, AlphaFold doesn't capture the conformal dynamics of their everchanging structure. IDPs have to still obey the laws of quantum mechanics, therefore, a large, accurate quantum computer would correctly predict its dynamics without having to have seen examples prior. (Note: there is a new startup Topos Bio which is aiming to tackle structure prediction models for IDPs by predicting conformal ensembles at an atomic level).<w>

![Quantum Computer](/assets/images/d41586-021-03476-5_19875844.jpg)

But the same drawbacks to analog computers of the past, rear their heads. Entangled qubits cannot be maintained in the presence of noise, and I mean any kind of noise. The slightest thermal, electrical, or mechanical disturbances breaks the quantum state. This is why quantum computers are often housed in massive, near-absolute-zero cooled chambers where virtually no noise can destroy delicate entanglements. Quantum error correction methods exist, however, its unclear whether the number of additional qubits needed to error correct for the qubits being used for calculations ultimately erases any performance gains. In other words, you may need an exponentially higher number of error-correcting qubits than computational qubits. There are attempts to utilize topological quantum matter which could maintain quantum states with better resistance to environmental perturbations possibly eliminating the need for error-correcting qubits or greatly reducing them. If this wasn't already enough of an engineering issue, its not know what implementaion of a qubit will allow for stable entanglement over long time horizons (ie seconds). Currently, the entanglement lasts in the 10s to 100s of microseconds. So while quantum is promising, there are tons of engineering issues that need to be figured out. I had a professor tell me once that quantum computing is to where digital computing was in the 1940s and 50s. We knew it was theorectically possible with swithing circuits, but no idea what shape or form the transistor and architecture would take. Simiarly, there are a vast number of competing qubit implementations and architectures none of which has been able to claim superiority.<w> 

![Thermodynamic Computer](/assets/images/41467_2025_59011_Fig1_HTML.png)

In the way that AI has convinced someone like Demis Hassabis that quantum computers are not necessary for simulating complex dynamics with accuracy, many hardware designers have pointed out that there are physics-based hardware that can be run at room temperature, is more robust against noise (or perhaps even utilizes noise in computations), and can be implemented with components that can be manufactured easily and for cheap. These have taken on a variety of different names: probabilistic computing, thermodynamic computing, unconventional computing, etc. There are also a flurry of startups in this space: Normal Computing, Extropic, Flucta, Unconventional AI (although they are looking more at mapping ODEs to circuits). The idea here has a familiar tune, you take some energy landscape you wish to minimize and map it onto a set of interacting components (in the picture above RLC oscillators with a noise source) with programmable connections, and the phase dynamics of the circuit falls into a decent local minimum of the landscape. Effectively, these are all forms of an Ising model borne out on physical hardware. With an intentional noise schedule and other techniques (parallel tempering, etc), you might even be able to find solutions close to a global minimum. I've got a soft spot for this area since my Master's research was working on mixed-signal Ising machines using digital ring oscillators and resistive coupling to solve combinatorial optimization problems (https://www.nature.com/articles/s41928-023-01021-y).<w>

_Sidenote: It looks like Normal's approach to computing has actually changed. They were originally doing coupled oscillator, Ising-like hardware, but have pivoted to something more aligned with stochastic computing (which also originated if I remember correctly at the University of Minnesota - Bazargan, Lilja, and Riedel). This is not really physics-based computing, instead you produce arbitrarily long sequences of 1s and 0s with some probability of the string being 1 and then use simple logic gates to perform complex operations on probabilities (AND gate for multiplication etc). In Normal's case, it seems like they are using ternary? (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.20883) Here's a good primer on stochastic computing - https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2429384.2429483_<w>

The Ising model, developed by Ernst Ising, is a simplified way to study ferromagnetism and phase transitions using statistical mechanics. Statistical mechanics can be thought of the physics of a lot of small things interacting with each other, like a gas. In this way, statistical mechanics is the "physics of thermodynamics" (thus, thermodynamic computing). Each atom is laid out in a 2D lattice and has some dipole spin. These dipoles are coupled to neighboring ones via magnetic fields. At high temperatures, the spins are disordered and as you lower the temperature they align in a way to minimize the total energy of the system. A simple case would be just two atoms side by side. Both would have to have opposite spins to be in its lowest energy state (kind of like how two bar magnets will force opposite ends to align). In high dimensional cases, the Ising model becomes equivalent to spin glasses (in the mean-field).<w> 

Anyways, you don't specifically need oscillators for probabilistic computing, as Kerem Camsari's hugely influential work in this space has been largely done using spintronic devices like magnetic tunnel junctions (MTJs). Instead of relying on phase drift as noise in your coupled oscillator system, MTJs exhibit a behavior where their outputs randomly jump between 0 and 1 and the time spent being in either state (probability) can be tuned using voltage. This is what Camsari termed the probabilistic bit or p-bit. Coupled oscillators or p-bit networks implement fundamentally the same Ising Hamiltonian, and their probabilistic nature allows them to sample from simple Boltzmann or Gaussian distributions without the need for Monte Carlo methods. This has a lot of useful applications, and those at Extropic have thought the idea of successively sampling from simple distributions allows for implementing an algorithm akin to diffusion (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.23972v1). Camsari's startup Flucta is brand new (founded with CMU's Tathagata Srimani), and they will likely reveal details around their architecture and application soon.<w>
